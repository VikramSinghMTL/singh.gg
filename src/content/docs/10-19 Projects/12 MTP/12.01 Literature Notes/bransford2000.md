## Key Ideas

- Experts are able to separate logical/related sections from the whole problem, otherwise known as _chunks_. These chunks can be configured in different ways by the expert when determining solutions to problems. Novices lack this ability and can only see the problem in individually scattered pieces. Another term to describe a logical chunk is _schema_. Experts use schemas to develop a strong understanding of the problem. ([Bransford et al. 2000:32](zotero://open-pdf/library/items/WLNJFA7X?page=3))
- The expert can use these schemas together with other schemas from co-related fields to convey concepts and link/connect ideas. ([Bransford et al. 2000:38](zotero://open-pdf/library/items/WLNJFA7X?page=9))
- The schemas an expert uses can be extended to be used in a learning context. Textbooks tend to focus on the lower levels of Bloom's Taxonomy (i.e. remember and understand) which cause students to stress about memorizing everything. Teachers should leverage common schemas used by experts (ex. design patterns or debugging techniques) in addition to the factual knowledge of the course content. ([Bransford et al. 2000:42](zotero://open-pdf/library/items/WLNJFA7X?page=13))
- The _conditionalization_ (i.e. contextualization) of knowledge means the ability to apply knowledge in situations that are not cut-and-dry. For example, students can answer questions at the end of a chapter because they know all the answers will involve the knowledge from the chapter. However, when faced with problems that are not adjacent to the relevant knowledge, they get tripped up. Experts are able to identify what knowledge to use when and where. ([Bransford et al. 2000:43](zotero://open-pdf/library/items/WLNJFA7X?page=14))
- Having the knowledge is one thing, being able to retrieve the knowledge when required is another. Knowledge retrieval can be categorized by _effortful_ (novice), _fluent_, and _automatic_ (expert). A novice driver has trouble keeping track of all the cars and signs on the road and cannot maintain a conversation while driving. An expert driver processes all the cars and signs on the road automatically and can maintain a conversation while driving. ([Bransford et al. 2000:44](zotero://open-pdf/library/items/WLNJFA7X?page=15))
- Just because one is an expert in their discipline does not mean that they will automatically be effective at teaching the discipline. Being an expert means it is likely that much time has passed since they were a novice, so they tend to forget the novice's perspective. What is required is _pedagogical content knowledge_ which combines the knowledge and skills of a discipline expert with the knowledge and skills of an expert teacher. ([Bransford et al. 2000:45](zotero://open-pdf/library/items/WLNJFA7X?page=16))
- Experts can be categorized into _artisans_ and _virtuosos_. Artisans are by-the-book and tend to label problems using predetermined buckets (i.e. inflexible). Virtuosos use the problems as jumping off points and try to come up with novel ways to approach and solve problems (i.e. flexible). ([Bransford et al. 2000:46](zotero://open-pdf/library/items/WLNJFA7X?page=17))
- Being aware of what one knows and where the gaps in their knowledge are can be referred to as _metacognition_ and is a characteristic of an expert. Novices are unable to distinguish between what they know and what they don't know. ([Bransford et al. 2000:47](zotero://open-pdf/library/items/WLNJFA7X?page=18)) ^f2ea4a

> "We close this chapter with two important cautionary notes. First, the six principles of expertise need to be considered simultaneously, as parts of an overall system. We divided our discussion into six points in order to facilitate explanation, but each point interacts with the others; this interrelationship has important educational implications. For example, the idea of promoting fluent access to knowledge (principle 4) must be approached with an eye toward helping students develop an understanding of the subject matter (principle 2), learn when, where and why to use information (principle 3), and learn to recognize meaningful patterns of information (principle 1). Furthermore, all these need to be approached from the perspective of helping students develop adaptive expertise (principle 6), which includes helping them become metacognitive about their learning so that they can assess their own progress and continually identify and pursue new learning goals. An example in mathematics is getting students to recognize when a proof is needed. Metacognition can help students develop personally relevant pedagogical content knowledge, analogous to the pedagogical content knowledge available to effective teachers (principle 5). In short, students need to develop the ability to teach themselves." ([Bransford et al. 2000:50](zotero://open-pdf/library/items/WLNJFA7X?page=21))
