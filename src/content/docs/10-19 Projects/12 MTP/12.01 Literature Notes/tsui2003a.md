# Annotations(2021-10-25)
“The teacher knows the social structure of the school and what it requires, of teacher and student, for survival and for success; she knows the community of which the school is a part, and has a sense of what it will and will not accept.” (Tsui, 2003, p. 3)
“she sees deliberative process and intuitive and reflective processes as equally important” (Tsui, 2003, p. 3)
“a distinction between procedural knowledge, the "knowing how," and declarative knowledge, the "knowing that."” (Tsui, 2003, p. 3)
“which see knowledge as contextually developed as practitioners respond to the specific context in which they operate.” (Tsui, 2003, p. 4) Situated knowledge.
“She found that just as in other forms of situated knowledge that is contextually developed, teacher knowledge is developed in the specific context of the school and classroom setting. And just as the former tends to make use of the characteristic features of the context when coming up with solutions, the knowledge that is embedded in the teaching act makes use of the features of the teaching situation, such as who the students are, what the classroom is like, the physical environment of the school, the time of the year and even the time of the day. In other words, this kind of knowledge is "embedded in the artifacts of a context"” (Tsui, 2003, p. 4)
“Pedagogical content knowledge refers to the representation of a subject by the use of analogies, examples, illustrations, explanations,” (Tsui, 2003, p. 5)
“and demonstrations in order to make it comprehensible to students. In order for a representation to be effective, teachers need to understand what makes a particular topic easy or difficult for students, what their preconceptions and misconceptions are, and what strategies are effective in dealing with their misconceptions.” (Tsui, 2003, p. 6)
