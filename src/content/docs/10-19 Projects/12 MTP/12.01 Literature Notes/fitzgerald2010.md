## Key Ideas

- Debugging is hard for novice programmers since it requires several other skills to do effectively. Students must know the bare minimum programming fundamentals, be able to read code and understand what the different instructions mean, and have a general idea of where the problem could be coming from. They must also be able to recall information, identify the correct context of the problem, and use programming constructs correctly. The authors hoped to analyze the differences between what students said they did versus what they actually did to identify gaps in their knowledge. ([Fitzgerald et al. 2010:390](zotero://open-pdf/library/items/2ATQG4V2?page=1))
- Fitzgerald et al. identified that much of the research about the debugging skills of students was done in the 1980's. Their study aimed to utilize strategies from those older studies using the context of modern programming environments during the Spring of 2007 at 6 universities in the United States and 1 university in the United Kingdom. The study involved students doing a programming exercise, debugging the program, participating in an interview, and filling out a debugging survey. ([Fitzgerald et al. 2010:391](zotero://open-pdf/library/items/2ATQG4V2?page=2))
- Fitzgerald et al. report that debugging requires strong problem-solving skills and strategies and that debugging should be explicitly taught by teachers. The students should be tasked with thinking about all the different ways the error could be happening before allowing them to alter the code. This would involve going back to the problem statement to nail down exactly what the desired output should be. Students must practice articulating between "desired output" and "actual output". To reduce cognitive overload, teachers should take care to only use one programming style (ex. naming conventions, brace placement, etc.). ([Fitzgerald et al. 2010:395](zotero://open-pdf/library/items/2ATQG4V2?page=6))
- Software testing and debugging go hand in hand. Teaching the students about good testing practices through live testing demonstrations can enable students to think about what the desired results should be. ([Fitzgerald et al. 2010:395](zotero://open-pdf/library/items/2ATQG4V2?page=6))
- Pair programming can allow students to collaborate on the debugging process together. By having to explain their thought process out loud to their peer, they are forced to create a mental trace model of how the code is being executed. This will also expose the student to a variety of approaches. ([Fitzgerald et al. 2010:395](zotero://open-pdf/library/items/2ATQG4V2?page=6))
- Students should be tasked with answering metacognitive questions like ^1637cb
  - Why did you make that change?
  - What do you think will happen now?
  ([Fitzgerald et al. 2010:395](zotero://open-pdf/library/items/2ATQG4V2?page=6))
- The authors posit that for extremely novice programmers, using the internet to search for solutions should be discouraged since these students can easily get lost in the plethora of information and resources that exist on the web. They conclude by stating that more research should be done about the novice programmer's use of the internet to solve problems. ([Fitzgerald et al. 2010:396](zotero://open-pdf/library/items/2ATQG4V2?page=7))
